SECTION 2: UNDERSTANDING INDIAN HANDICRAFTS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

India has over 7 million artisans across 23 major craft categories. The
handicraft sector generates over ₹4.5 lakh crore annually and supports the
livelihoods of approximately 200 million people including weavers, potters,
metal casters, painters, and embroidery workers.

Major craft categories in India:
1. Textiles and Weaving: Silk sarees, cotton handlooms, woollen shawls
2. Embroidery: Chikankari, Phulkari, Kantha, Kashmiri embroidery
3. Painting: Madhubani, Pattachitra, Warli, Gond, Tanjore, Kalamkari
4. Metal Craft: Bidriware, Dhokra, Dokra, Bronze casting, Aranmula Kannadi
5. Pottery: Blue Pottery, Khurja pottery, Terracotta, Longpi pottery
6. Wood Craft: Channapatna toys, Sandalwood carving, Rosewood inlay
7. Stonework: Mahabalipuram sculptures, Agra marble inlay, Rajasthan stonework
8. Leather Craft: Kolhapuri chappal, Mojari footwear
9. Tribal Art: Warli, Gond, Madhubani, Sohrai, Bhil painting
10. Paper Craft: Papier-mâché (Kashmir), Kagzi pottery

SECTION 3: GEOGRAPHICAL INDICATION (GI) TAGS — COMPLETE GUIDE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

A Geographical Indication (GI) tag is a sign used on products that have a
specific geographical origin and possess qualities or reputation due to that
origin. In India, GI tags are governed by the Geographical Indications of
Goods (Registration and Protection) Act, 1999.

Benefits of GI tag for artisans:
- Legal protection against imitation and misuse
- Better market access and premium pricing
- Brand recognition nationally and internationally
- Protection from cheap machine-made imitations
- Eligibility for government grants and schemes

How to get GI tag:
- Apply through the Geographical Indications Registry, Chennai
- Application fee: ₹5,000 per class
- Process takes 1-3 years
- Requires documentation of historical origin and production process
- Support available from state handicraft departments

PM VISHWAKARMA SCHEME (2023-2028):
The Government of India launched PM Vishwakarma on September 17, 2023 with
a budget of ₹13,000 crore over 5 years.

Who is eligible: 18 categories of traditional artisans and craftspeople including
carpenters (Badhai), boat makers, armourers, blacksmiths (Lohar), hammer and
tool kit makers, locksmiths, goldsmiths (Sonar), potters (Kumhar), sculptors,
cobblers (Mochi/Charmakar), masons (Rajmistri), basket/mat weavers, doll and toy
makers, barbers (Naai), garland makers (Maalakar), washermen (Dhobi), tailors
(Darzi), and fishing net makers.

Benefits under PM Vishwakarma:
- Recognition as Vishwakarma with PM Vishwakarma Certificate and ID Card
- Skill Upgrade: 5-7 day basic training + 15 day advanced training with ₹500/day stipend
- Toolkit Incentive: ₹15,000 e-voucher for purchasing modern tools
- Collateral-free Credit: Loans up to ₹1 lakh (first tranche) @ 5% interest
  and up to ₹2 lakh (second tranche) @ 5% interest
- Digital Transaction Incentive: ₹1 per transaction up to 100 transactions/month
- Marketing Support: Access to ONDC, GeM portal, trade fairs

How to apply: Visit PM Vishwakarma portal (pmvishwakarma.gov.in) or nearest CSC
(Common Service Centre). Registration is free. Need Aadhaar and mobile number.

NATIONAL HANDLOOM DEVELOPMENT PROGRAMME (NHDP):
Implemented by Ministry of Textiles. Provides financial assistance for:
- Procurement of looms and accessories
- Yarn supply at subsidized rates
- Cluster development
- Marketing support through Handloom Haat
- Design development workshops
- e-Commerce assistance

Budget: ₹2,031 crore for 2021-2026

HANDLOOM WEAVERS' COMPREHENSIVE WELFARE SCHEME:
- Mahatma Gandhi Bunkar Bima Yojana: Insurance coverage for weavers
  (Life cover ₹2 lakh, accidental death ₹2 lakh)
- Health Insurance: Rashtriya Swasthya Bima Yojana for weavers
- Credit: Credit Guarantee Trust for Micro Enterprises (CGTME)

NATIONAL HANDICRAFTS DEVELOPMENT PROGRAMME (NHDP):
Run by Development Commissioner (Handicrafts), Ministry of Textiles.

Key components:
1. Mega Cluster Development: 6 mega clusters across India (Varanasi, Mirzapur,
   Bhadohi carpet, Kashmir, Kutch, Moradabad)
2. Ambedkar Hastshilp Vikas Yojana: Comprehensive skill development for SC/ST artisans
3. Design & Technology Upgradation: Collaborations with NID (National Institute of Design)
4. Marketing Events: Dilli Haat, Surajkund Mela, International trade fairs
5. Recognition: National Award, Shilp Guru Award, Presidential Award for weavers

SECTION 5: STATE-WISE CRAFT HERITAGE (DETAILED)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

RAJASTHAN:
Heritage overview: Rajasthan is India's largest state by area and home to one
of the richest craft traditions in the world, shaped by the patronage of Rajput
kings, Mughal emperors, and trade routes through the Thar desert.

Key crafts:
1. Blue Pottery (Jaipur): Not made from clay but from quartz powder, glass,
   Multani mitti, borax, and sodium sulphate. Fired at low temperatures.
   Persian origin, brought to Jaipur by Kachhwaha Rajput rulers. GI-tagged.
   Best seen at: Kripal Kumbh, Jaipur; Neemrana complex.

2. Sanganer Block Printing: Hand-carved wooden blocks stamped onto cotton/silk.
   Originated in Sanganer village near Jaipur. Uses natural vegetable dyes.
   Famous for: floral and geometric motifs. GI-tagged.

3. Bagru Block Printing: Uses chemical resists (dabu — a mud resist paste).
   More earthy colours than Sanganer. Village 30km from Jaipur.

4. Bandhani (Tie-Dye): Tiny dots tied with thread before dyeing. Practiced in
   Jaipur, Jodhpur, Barmer. Takes months for fine work. Used in odhna,
   dupatta, and sarees. Kutch (Gujarat) also famous for this.

5. Thewa Art: Gold foil designs fused onto glass. Unique to Pratapgarh district.
   One family (Raj Soni) holds the secret of the 17th-century technique.
   UNESCO documented as intangible cultural heritage.

6. Meenakari: Enamel work on gold or silver jewellery. Jaipur is the global
   centre for this Mughal-era craft. Distinctive use of ruby red enamel.

7. Kota Doria: Ultra-light cotton-silk sarees from Kota district. Named for their
   chequered texture (khat pattern). So light you can see through them.
   GI-tagged.

Craft clusters: Jaipur (blue pottery, gems, textiles), Barmer (tie-dye, leather),
Jaisalmer (stone carving), Udaipur (miniature painting), Kota (Doria sarees).

UNESCO recognition: Jaipur is a UNESCO Creative City of Crafts and Folk Art.

GUJARAT:
Heritage overview: Gujarat's 1600km coastline and Silk Road trade connections
shaped an extraordinarily diverse craft tradition — from fine silk weaving to
mirror-work embroidery.

Key crafts:
1. Patan Patola: Double-ikat silk saree where both warp AND weft threads are
   resist-dyed before weaving. Only 3 families in Patan (Salvi community) know
   this technique. A single saree takes 4-6 months. Starts at ₹1.5 lakh.
   UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage. GI-tagged.

2. Kutch Embroidery: Several distinct styles — Ahir, Rabari, Mutwa, Jat —
   each community has its own colour palette and stitch vocabulary.
   Uses mirrors (shisha work) extensively. Village Bhujodi near Bhuj is the
   craft hub.

3. Tangaliya Weaving: A special weave creating raised dot patterns on wool.
   Practiced by Dangasiya Koli community in Surendranagar district.
   GI-tagged. Almost extinct — fewer than 25 active weavers.

4. Rogan Painting: Castor oil-based paint drawn with a stylus on cloth.
   Unique to Nirona village, Kutch. One family (Khatri family) practises it.
   Barack Obama received a Rogan painting from PM Modi in 2014.

5. Ajrakh Printing: Resist block printing using natural clay, turmeric, alum.
   Practised by Khatri community in Dhamadka, Kutch.
   Geometric Islamic patterns. Takes 14-16 steps including river washing.

6. Bandhani: Jamnagar and Bhuj are centres. Used in wedding trousseau.
   Fine Bandhani can have 75,000+ dots on a single saree.

Craft clusters: Kutch (embroidery, Rogan, Ajrakh, Bandhani), Patan (Patola),
Rajkot (Bandhani), Bhavnagar (wood carving), Vadodara (Pichwai painting).

UTTAR PRADESH:
Heritage overview: As the seat of Mughal power, UP developed some of India's
most refined craft traditions — Banarasi silk weaving, Chikankari embroidery,
and marble inlay work at Agra.

Key crafts:
1. Chikankari (Lucknow): 400-year-old Mughal court embroidery tradition.
   36 traditional stitches including murri (French knot), phanda, banarsi,
   shadow work, keel kangan. Done on fine muslin, georgette, or silk.
   4,000+ families practice it in Lucknow. Mostly women artisans.
   GI-tagged. Market size: ₹3,000+ crore annually.

2. Banarasi Silk (Varanasi): Gold and silver zari (metallic thread) brocade.
   Patterns include butis (small motifs), jangla (jungle), tanchoi, and
   meenakari. A genuine Banarasi takes weeks to months on a jacquard loom.
   GI-tagged. Protected by SILK MARK certification. ₹5,000 to ₹5+ lakh per saree.

3. Agra Marble Inlay (Pietra Dura): Semi-precious stones (malachite, lapis
   lazuli, mother of pearl) inlaid in white marble. Originated in Mughal era
   for the Taj Mahal. Still practiced by 10,000+ families in Agra.

4. Bhadohi & Mirzapur Carpet: Hand-knotted wool and silk carpets. Bhadohi
   district is world's carpet capital — exports to USA, Germany, UK.
   GI-tagged. Uses Persian and Turkish knots.

5. Khurja Pottery: Blue and white ceramic tableware and tiles from Khurja
   town in Bulandshahr. Also called "Khurja pottery". Modern designs,
   food-safe glazes. Major export item.

6. Saharanpur Wood Carving: Intricate filigree carving in sheesham (rosewood)
   and teak. Furniture, decorative items. Centre near Saharanpur city.

WEST BENGAL:
Key crafts:
1. Baluchari Saree (Bishnupur): Silk sarees with narrative panels woven into
   the pallu depicting scenes from Ramayana, Mahabharata, Mughal court life.
   Named after Baluchar village in Murshidabad. Revived after near-extinction
   in 1950s by Subhas Chandra Das. GI-tagged.

2. Nakshi Kantha: Embroidered quilt using old saris. Running stitch patterns
   depict stories, landscapes, religious motifs. Made by women in rural Bengal.
   GI-tagged.

3. Dokra (Bankura): Lost-wax brass casting. Non-lit single-use mould process.
   Tribal craft of the Lodha and Santar communities. Horses, elephants,
   jewellery, lamps. Each piece unique. From Bikna village, Bankura.

4. Terracotta of Bishnupur: Terracotta temples and plaques depicting Krishna-
   Leela and Ramayana scenes. Bishnupur temples are architectural masterpieces.
   Modern artisans create miniature plaques and decorative items.

5. Madhur Kathi Mat: Woven from screw-pine grass. Water-resistant, food-safe.
   Used as prayer mats, table mats. From Sabang area, Medinipur.

ODISHA:
Key crafts:
1. Pattachitra: Cloth painting using natural colours on a hardened cloth
   (patta) coated with chalk and gum. Depicts Lord Jagannath, Gita Govinda,
   and folk tales. Raghurajpur village near Puri — India's first heritage
   crafts village. GI-tagged. Requires 5-15 natural pigments made from
   minerals, plants, and earth.

2. Ikat (Tie & Dye Weaving): Both single ikat (Odisha) and double ikat
   (Sambalpuri). Vibrant geometric patterns. Major weaving hubs: Sambalpur,
   Bargarh, Sonepur, Bolangir. GI-tagged as "Odisha Ikat".

3. Dhokra (Bastar-style): Similar to West Bengal's Dokra but Odisha has its
   own tradition in tribal belts. Tribal motifs, animals, deities.

4. Appliqué (Pipili): Colourful fabric appliqué for chanua (canopies),
   garden umbrellas, lamp shades, and decorative items. Pipili village on the
   Puri-Bhubaneswar highway. Famous for Rath Yatra canopies.

5. Silver Filigree (Cuttack): Intricate silver wire work — Tarakasi.
   Cuttack city famous for silver Goddess Durga idols for Durga Puja.

MADHYA PRADESH:
Key crafts:
1. Chanderi Fabric (Chanderi, Ashok Nagar): Lightweight silk-cotton blend with
   gold zari. 700-year-old tradition. Butis, peacock, and floral motifs.
   GI-tagged. Chanderis are so fine they're called "woven air".

2. Maheshwari Saree (Maheshwar, Khargone): Cotton-silk blend with distinctive
   reversible border. Created under patronage of Queen Ahilyabai Holkar in
   the 18th century. Features: 9-yard and 5-yard versions. GI-tagged.

3. Bagh Print (Bagh, Dhar): Traditional block printing using natural dyes
   (alizarin, tamarind seed powder) on cotton. Bold geometric patterns in
   red and black. Takes 14 days per piece including river washing.
   GI-tagged. Named after Bagh river.

4. Gond Painting (Mandla, Baiga areas): Tribal painting tradition using
   dots and dashes to create elaborate depictions of forest life, spirits,
   and community narratives. Jangarh Singh Shyam (Padma Shri) is the father
   of modern Gond art. Now on international exhibition circuits.

5. Pichwai Painting (Nathdwara, though now in Rajasthan by geography,
   origin tied to Braj/Mewar): Painted behind shrine of Lord Srinath ji.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

KERALA:
Key crafts:
1. Aranmula Kannadi (Pathanamthitta): Surface reflection metal mirror made
   from a secret alloy of copper and tin. Unlike glass mirrors, reflects from
   surface. Unique in the world. 12 families in Aranmula know the formula.
   GI-tagged 2004. Each takes 3-5 days to cast and polish.
   Price: ₹5,000 to ₹1 lakh+ depending on size.

2. Kerala Mural Painting: Traditionally found on temple walls. Bold outlines,
   primary colours. Subjects: Dashavataram, Ramayana, Mahabharata.
   Training centres in Thrissur. GI-tagged.

3. Coir Products (Alappuzha): From coconut fibre — mats, mattresses, rope.
   Alappuzha (Alleppey) is the world's coir capital. Women-dominated industry.

4. Kasavu Saree (Kerala Saree): Cream/white cotton with gold kasavu (zari)
   border. Worn for Onam. Balaramapuram near Thiruvananthapuram is the hub.

5. Screw Pine Craft (Pandanus leaf weaving): Baskets, mats, bags. Coastal
   Kerala. UNESCO-documented tradition.

6. Nirmalyam Flowers: Artistic flower arrangements for temple festivals.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

TAMIL NADU:
Key crafts:
1. Kancheepuram Silk (Kancheepuram): The "Queen of Silks". Pure mulberry silk
   with heavy zari (gold thread from Surat). Zari content 50-75% of saree
   weight. Three-shuttle weaving technique. GI-tagged. A bridal Kancheepuram
   can cost ₹25,000 to ₹5 lakh. 50,000+ weavers.

2. Thanjavur Painting: Classical South Indian painting with raised gold
   embossing using chalk powder, glue, and gold leaf. Depicts Hindu deities
   with semi-precious stones. Thanjavur district. GI-tagged.

3. Mahabalipuram Stone Sculpture: Shore temple town is a live craft cluster.
   Second-generation sculptors create from pink granite and black granite.
   Mahabalipuram is the only UNESCO World Heritage Site with a live craft
   tradition. GI-tagged.

4. Chettinad Saree (Karaikudi area): Cotton sarees with distinctive temple
   borders. Named after the Chettinad region.

5. Toda Embroidery (Nilgiris): Geometric embroidery by the Toda tribe.
   Red and black on white cotton. UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage.
   GI-tagged. Only 700 Toda people remain.

6. Eththalkudi Mat: Woven on traditional loom from Calamus (cane) grass.
   Pattamadai village, Tirunelveli. GI-tagged.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

KARNATAKA:
Key crafts:
1. Mysore Silk (Mysuru): Pure silk sarees with gold zari. Karnataka Silk
   Industries Corporation (KSIC) produces government-certified silk.
   Mysore Palace connection — silk weaving revived by Wodeyar kings.
   GI-tagged. Mysore Silk is identified by the KSIC stamp.

2. Bidriware (Bidar): Zinc alloy (95% zinc, 5% copper) inlaid with fine silver
   wire. Unique matte black finish achieved using mud from the Bidar fort.
   14th-century origin from Bahamani Sultanate. GI-tagged.

3. Channapatna Toys (Ramnagara): Lacquered wooden toys using ivory-wood
   (Wrightia tinctoria) and natural vegetable dyes. Safe for children.
   Tipu Sultan invited Persian craftsmen to start this craft. GI-tagged.
   Channapatna is called "Toy Town of India".

4. Kasuti Embroidery: Traditional Karnataka needlework using running stitch.
   Negi, Gavanti, Murgi, Menthi — four main stitch types.
   Featured on traditional Ilkal and Dharwad sarees.

5. Ilkal Saree: Distinct saree from Ilkal town, Bagalkote district.
   Cotton body with art silk pallu. Attached pallu technique.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

MAHARASHTRA:
Key crafts:
1. Warli Painting (Palghar district): Tribal painting of the Warli tribe using
   white rice paste circles, triangles, and squares on red-ochre backgrounds.
   Depicts daily life, hunting, harvest, and marriage rituals.
   Jivya Soma Mashe (Padma Shri) brought it to international attention in 1970s.
   GI-tagged.

2. Paithani Saree (Paithan, Aurangabad): Silk sarees with tapestry-woven zari
   peacock and lotus motifs in the pallu. 2000-year-old tradition.
   Named after the ancient city of Pratishthana (modern Paithan).
   A pure Paithani takes 6 months to 2 years. Price: ₹30,000 to ₹5 lakh.
   GI-tagged.

3. Kolhapuri Chappal: Hand-stitched leather sandals from Kolhapur.
   Vegetable-tanned leather, 12-step process. Durable — lasts 20+ years.
   GI-tagged (shared with Karnataka).

4. Nashik Silver Jewellery: Tribal silver jewellery traditions of Nashik's
   Adivasi communities.

5. Sawantwadi Royal Crafts: Painted wooden toys and lacquerware from
   Sawantwadi kingdom. Animals, birds, chess sets.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PUNJAB:
Key crafts:
1. Phulkari (Amritsar, Ludhiana, Bathinda): "Flower work" — vibrant embroidery
   on coarse cotton (khaddar) using floss silk. Darning stitch from wrong side.
   Types: Bagh (full cover), Vari-da-bagh (bridal), Chope (gifted at weddings).
   GI-tagged. Market size: ₹500 crore+ annually.

2. Leather Craft (Jallandhar): Sports goods, shoes, bags. India's sports goods
   export hub.

3. Wooden Furniture (Hoshiarpur): Intricate carved wood furniture and inlay work.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

ASSAM:
Key crafts:
1. Muga Silk: World's only naturally golden-coloured wild silk. Produced by
   Antheraea assamensis silkworm that feeds on Som and Sualu leaves.
   Unique to Assam — cannot be replicated elsewhere. Sualkuchi is the weaving
   hub (called "Manchester of the East"). GI-tagged.

2. Eri Silk (Ahimsa Silk): Produced without killing the silkworm. Coarse,
   warm silk. Used for shawls and muga mix fabrics. Buddhist and Jain
   communities prefer it.

3. Pat Silk: Fine white silk. Woven into traditional mekhela-chador (two-piece
   Assamese women's garment).

4. Bell Metal Work (Sarthebari): Crafted by the Oriya community in Barpeta
   district. Platters, bowls, glasses, ritual vessels. Intricate lathed finish.

5. Bamboo & Cane Craft: Assam is India's bamboo basket. Traditional
   storage containers, furniture, musical instruments.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

HIMACHAL PRADESH:
Key crafts:
1. Kullu Shawl: Woollen shawls with geometric temple-border (devta patti)
   design. Made on traditional frame looms using local sheep wool and
   Angora hair. Kullu valley is the production centre. GI-tagged 2004.
   Exported to Germany, USA, Japan.

2. Chamba Rumal: Embroidered handkerchief with scenes from epics.
   Double-satin stitch, looks same on both sides. Royal tradition from
   Chamba kingdom. GI-tagged.

3. Kangra Painting (Pahari): Miniature painting school of the Kangra valley.
   18th-century golden age under Sansar Chand. Subjects: Radha-Krishna
   love stories, Ramayana, portrait painting. Delicate, lyrical style.

4. Kinnauri Shawl (Kinnaur): Woollen shawls with distinctive geometric
   patterns woven on backstrap looms at high altitude. Made by women
   artisans. Includes traditional belt-weaving.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

JAMMU & KASHMIR:
Key crafts:
1. Kashmir Pashmina: Ultra-fine cashmere wool from Changthangi goat of
   Ladakh region. Fibre diameter 12-16 microns (human hair: 70 microns).
   Hand-spun and hand-woven in Srinagar. GI-tagged 2008.
   Types: Plain, Kani (tapestry weave), Sozni (embroidered).
   A Kani shawl (all hand-woven tapestry) takes 6-18 months for one piece.
   Price: ₹10,000 to ₹10 lakh.

2. Kani Shawl: Designed using wooden bobbins (kani/cane sticks) instead of
   normal shuttle. Each colour thread managed separately. Creates tapestry-
   like woven designs. GI-tagged. UNESCO documented.

3. Sozni Embroidery (Kashmir Sozni Craft): Needlework on Pashmina or silk.
   Delicate floral patterns (chenar leaf, almond, chinar flower).
   GI-tagged 2011.

4. Kashmiri Paper Machie: Papier-mâché decorative items with intricate
   hand-painted floral designs in Persian/Mughal style. GI-tagged.
   Walnut shells, chinar leaves as moulds.

5. Hand-Knotted Carpet (Kashmiri Carpet): Persian-origin carpets with floral
   medallion designs. Up to 400 knots per square inch. GI-tagged.
   Taking 3-5 years for a large room carpet.

6. Walnut Wood Carving: Deep relief carving on Kashmiri walnut.
   Furniture, decorative panels, trays. GI-tagged.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

BIHAR:
Key crafts:
1. Madhubani Painting: Ancient painting tradition of the Mithila region.
   Originally done by women on the walls of homes (kohbar ghar) for marriages.
   Styles: Bharni (filled colour), Kachni (line work), Godna (tattoo-inspired),
   Tantrik, Kohbar. Made on cloth, paper, canvas with natural and chemical colours.
   GI-tagged 2007. International recognition since the 1970s.

2. Sujini Embroidery (Bhusura, Muzaffarpur): Running stitch embroidery on
   old cotton fabric. Social commentary — depicts daily life, nature.
   GI-tagged.

3. Sikki Grass Work: Golden grass (Eulaliopsis binata) woven into figures,
   jewelry boxes, toys. Women artisans. GI-tagged.

4. Manjusha Art (Bhagalpur): Snake-worship painting with episodic narrative
   panels. Unique sequential story-telling format.

5. Tikuli Art: Lacquer painting on glass. Named after tikuli (bindi).
   Patna-based art. Being revived by government and NGOs.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

ANDHRA PRADESH:
Key crafts:
1. Kalamkari (Srikalahasti & Machilipatnam): Two distinct styles:
   a) Srikalahasti: Hand-drawn with bamboo pen (kalam), fermented iron solution.
      Scenes from Ramayana, Mahabharata. Traditional temple art.
   b) Machilipatnam: Block-printed using hand-carved wooden blocks.
      Persian-influenced floral and geometric patterns.
   Both GI-tagged 2009.

2. Kondapalli Toys (Kondapalli, Krishna dist): Wooden toys from tella poniki
   wood (white sander). Light, easy to carve. Brightly painted.
   Nativity sets, animal sets. GI-tagged 2009.

3. Uppada Jamdani Saree (East Godavari): Extra-weft patterning on silk.
   Geometric floral patterns. Uppada village on the coast. GI-tagged 2011.
   Lightweight, traditionally worn by bride during wedding.

4. Venkatagiri Saree: Fine cotton with zari border from Nellore district.
   Royal patronage of Velugoti kings.

5. Bobbili Veena (Bobbili, Vizianagaram): Handcrafted veena instruments.
   One of few remaining veena-making towns. GI-tagged in progress.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

MANIPUR:
Key crafts:
1. Moirangphee (Bishnupur): Sacred shawl of the Meitei community. Woven on
   loin loom with natural silk. Distinctive stripe patterns in red-black or
   red-white. Associated with pre-Hindu Sanamahism religion.
   Worn at Lai Haraoba festival. GI-tagged 2013.

2. Wangkhei Phee: Fine silk fabric from Wangkhei locality, Imphal.
   Used for traditional Meitei dress. GI-tagged.

3. Longpi Pottery (Ukhrul): Made from a special stone-clay mix found only
   in Longpi village of Ukhrul district. Nagaland-Manipur border area.
   No wheel used — entirely hand-shaped. Black in colour after firing.

4. Kauna Mat: Reed mat woven from kauna (Typha elephantina) — a water reed
   from Loktak Lake. Used for sleeping, prayer, and decorative purposes.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

UTTARAKHAND:
Key crafts:
1. Aipan (Kumaon): Sacred geometric folk art drawn with white rice paste on
   red ochre background by Kumaoni women. Drawn on floors, walls, and papers
   for every ritual occasion (Harela, Diwali, weddings, birth, death).
   Patterns include: Sur, Dharti, Choki, Shiv-shakti. GI-tagged 2019.

2. Ringal Craft (Bamboo craft): Mountain bamboo (Ringal) woven into baskets,
   kitchen implements, furniture. Pahari hill traditions.

3. Thulma Shawl: Heavy woollen shawl from Pithoragarh with geometric patterns.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

CHHATTISGARH:
Key crafts:
1. Dhokra (Bastar): Lost-wax (cire perdue) casting of brass. One of the world's
   oldest metal casting techniques (4000+ years). Clay core, beeswax model,
   clay outer mould — molten brass poured in — mould broken.
   Unique, non-repeatable pieces. Tribal Ghadwa community in Bastar.
   Subjects: Tribal deities, elephants, musicians, ritual objects.

2. Bastar Iron Craft: Tribal blacksmithing tradition from Bastar. Stylised
   iron trees, human figures, animals. GI-tagged 2021.

3. Bell Metal Craft: Similar to Assam's bell metal. Ritual vessels.

4. Godna (Tattoo) Art: Traditional body tattoo patterns of Bastar tribes
   now transferred to canvas and fabric by women artisans.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

JHARKHAND:
Key crafts:
1. Sohrai Khovar Painting (Hazaribagh): Among the world's oldest living art
   traditions — cave paintings discovered in Hazaribagh date to 10,000 BCE.
   Modern form: white geometric/animal paintings on mud walls using natural
   earth pigments (geru, kaolin, manganese). Done at harvest (Sohrai) and
   wedding (Khovar) festivals. GI-tagged 2019.

2. Dokra (Jharkhand): Similar to Bengal Dokra. Tribal communities in Singhbhum,
   Saraikela, Kharsawan districts.

3. Kosa Silk (Chaibasa): Rich wild silk from Chaibasa area. GI-tagged.
   Used in traditional tribal attire.

SECTION 6: ARTISAN CERTIFICATION & VERIFICATION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PEHCHAN CARD (Artisan Identity Card):
Issued by O/o Development Commissioner for Handicrafts, Ministry of Textiles.
Provides identity to artisans. Enables access to all government schemes.
Apply at: handicrafts.gov.in or nearest Shilpgram.

GI CERTIFICATE: Issued by GI Registry, Chennai to registered users of GI tags.

UDYAM REGISTRATION: For artisans operating as micro/small enterprises.
Replaces earlier Udyog Aadhaar. Free registration at udyamregistration.gov.in.

SILK MARK: For genuine silk products. Issued by Silk Mark Organisation of India.
Prevents fake silk being sold as genuine.

HANDLOOM MARK: For genuine handloom products.
Issued by Ministry of Textiles. Blue diamond-shaped tag.

INDIA HANDMADE MARK: Certifies genuinely hand-crafted products.

SECTION 8: CRAFT TOURISM & ARTISAN VISIT ETIQUETTE

Best craft villages to visit in India:
1. Raghurajpur (Odisha): Heritage craft village, 100% artisan families. Pattachitra.
2. Pipili (Odisha): Appliqué craft village.
3. Channapatna (Karnataka): Toy town, you can watch live toy-making.
4. Bhujodi (Gujarat): Weaving village near Bhuj, Kutch.
5. Nirona (Gujarat): Rogan painting — one family remaining.
6. Jitwarpur & Ranti (Bihar): Madhubani painting villages.
7. Raghurajpur (Puri, Odisha): UNESCO heritage craft village.
8. Bishnupur (West Bengal): Terracotta temples and crafts.
9. Kutch (Gujarat): Multiple craft clusters — embroidery, Ajrakh, Bandhani.
10. Aranmula (Kerala): Aranmula Kannadi metal mirrors.

Visiting artisan workshops:
- Book through CraftTrail for verified workshops
- Call ahead — artisans often travel to exhibitions
- Bring cash — not all artisans have card machines
- Ask before photographing — respect privacy
- Do not bargain aggressively — these are skilled professionals
- Consider buying directly — more money goes to the artisan
- Participate in workshops — most charge reasonable fees
- Leave reviews to help other travellers find genuine artisans

SECTION 9: FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

Q: How can I tell if a Pashmina shawl is genuine?
A: Genuine Kashmir Pashmina has GI tag, SILK MARK, and is hand-spun and
hand-woven. It is incredibly soft and warm. Price below ₹5,000 is suspicious.
Ring test: genuine Pashmina passes through a ring. Burn test: genuine Pashmina
smells like burning hair (protein fibre), not plastic. Buy from GI-certified
shops or directly from Sufiyan Brothers or reputed Srinagar shawl makers.

Q: What is the difference between Chikankari and regular embroidery?
A: Chikankari uses 36 distinct traditional stitches, most worked from the
wrong side of the fabric. The defining stitches are murri (French knot),
phanda, banarsi, and shadow work. Authentic Chikankari is done by hand on
fine muslin or georgette in Lucknow. Machine Chikankari exists but lacks
texture and subtlety.

Q: Which Indian craft is most endangered?
A: Several are critically endangered: Rogan painting (1 family in Nirona, Kutch),
Tangaliya weaving (25 weavers in Surendranagar), Thewa art (1 family in
Pratapgarh, Rajasthan), Toda embroidery (700 Toda people), Longpi pottery,
Kani shawl weaving (very few masters).

Q: How do I book a workshop with an artisan through CraftTrail?
A: Visit the artisan's profile on CraftTrail, click "Book Workshop", select
your date and group size, pay online. You'll receive confirmation via email
and WhatsApp. The artisan is notified and you visit their workshop on the
scheduled date.

Q: What is a trust score on CraftTrail?
A: Trust score (0-100) is calculated from: verified government documents (tier 1),
endorsements from craft experts and NGOs (tier 2), and visitor reviews (tier 3).
Scores above 80 indicate verified master artisans. Above 90 indicates
nationally recognized craftspeople.

Q: Are government schemes available for women artisans specifically?
A: Yes. PM Vishwakarma covers all genders equally. Additional schemes:
Mahila Samridhi Yojana, SHG (Self Help Group) support under DAY-NRLM
(Deen Dayal Antyodaya Yojana - National Rural Livelihoods Mission),
Handloom Weavers' Welfare Scheme has special provisions for women.
TRIFED specifically targets tribal women artisans.

Q: What is the difference between Dokra and Dhokra?
A: Same craft, different spelling. Dhokra is the more common spelling.
It refers to the non-ferrous metal casting using the lost-wax technique
practised by the Dhokra Damar tribe. Present in West Bengal (Bankura,
Midnapore), Odisha, Chhattisgarh (Bastar), and Jharkhand. Regional
styles differ slightly in motifs and alloy composition.

Q: Which Indian state has the most GI-tagged crafts?
A: Uttar Pradesh leads with 67+ GI tags (as of 2025), followed by
Karnataka with 40+, Tamil Nadu with 35+, Kerala with 30+, and
Rajasthan with 28+.

Q: Can foreigners buy GI-tagged products and take them out of India?
A: Yes, GI-tagged craft products can be legally exported. There is no restriction
on quantity for personal use. Commercial export requires standard customs
declarations. Antiques (100+ years old) require Archaeological Survey of India
clearance. Ivory, certain wildlife products, and cultural property are restricted.
